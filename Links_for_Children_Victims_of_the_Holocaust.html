German invasion of Poland in September 1939 set off a series of events that forever altered the Jewish community of Lodz, Poland, one of the largest in Europe. Despite the Germans’ violent oppression and imposed squalor and hunger, the community leadership strove to create a “normal” world for the children as long as it could. Give Me Your Children:  Voices from the Lodz Ghetto

http://www.ushmm.org/museum/exhibit/online/lodz/

In   New York City, January 2002, Lola Rein met with a curator of the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum. Lola told the story of her lonely survival during the Holocaust. At the end of the interview she reached into her bag and took out this tiny dress, handing over the only item directly linking her to her mother. Lola had spent seven months hiding in a hole in a ground, wearing only this dress sewn by her mother. It was her only possession. Learn more about this silent witness.   (from site) Includes video, images, text, testimony.

Children of the Holocaust (FCIT)

http://fcit.usf.edu/holocaust/people/children.htm

A Special 10th Anniversary Exhibition, explores the history of children who went underground to escape Nazi persecution. With identities disguised, and often physically concealed from the outside world, these youngsters faced constant fear, dilemmas, and danger. Theirs was a life in shadows, where a careless remark, a denunciation, or the murmurings of inquisitive neighbors could lead to discovery and death.   (from site) Includes study guide, bibliography, exhibition script, links to more information, artifacts and video testimony.

Life in Shadows:  Hidden Children and the Holocaust

http://www.ushmm.org/museum/exhibit/online/hiddenchildren/

Between the ages of 13 and 15, Anne Frank wrote short stories, fairy tales, essays, and the beginnings of a novel. Five notebooks and more than 300 loose pages, meticulously handwritten during her two years in hiding, survived the war. Launch the exhibition ‘An Unfinished Story’ to reveal the original writings—through sound and images—of a young woman who had great ambition to be a writer and was exploring her craft. (from site) Includes interviews, original writings, links, animated exhibit with images.   

Anne Frank the Writer:  An Unfinished Story

http://www.ushmm.org/museum/exhibit/online/af/htmlsite/

When World War II ended in 1945, six million European Jews were dead, including more than one million Jewish children. All Jews were targeted for death, but children were among the most vulnerable victims of the Nazi regime and its collaborators. For Holocaust Remembrance Day 2007, the Museum designated “Children in Crisis: Voices from the Holocaust” as the focus for the observance. (from site) Includes article, links to more information, and speeches from the 2007 Day of Remembrance.

Children of the Holocaust

http://www.museumoftolerance.com/site/c.tmL6KfNVLtH/b.5759983/k.6B50/Children_of_the_Holocaust/apps/nl/newsletter2.asp

"Until recently, the story of the children of the Holocaust was rarely told.  Here are accounts of the the war-time experiences of three child survivors: 5-year-old Lili, who was separated from her family and hidden in a convent; 7-year-old Krystyna, who lived for 14 months in a sewer, never seeing the light of day; and 14-year-old Alexander, who learned about death on a fateful day in the ghetto. These survivors speak for their friends and siblings -- the one-and-a-half million children who were murdered during the Holocaust."  (from site)
